Can Waxworms Help Solve Plastic Pollution?

Exploring how waxworm genetics may help break down plastic waste

🌍

The Plastic Problem

📈

Plastic builds up faster than we can remove it

⏰
400+

Years for plastic to break down naturally

🐢

Harms ecosystems and wildlife worldwide

⚠️

Why Should We Care?

🐻
Affects animals – Wildlife mistake plastic for food, causing injury and death
🍎
Enters food systems – Microplastics contaminate our food and water
👥
Impacts humans – Plastic particles found in human blood and organs
♻️
Recycling isn't enough – Less than 10% of plastic gets recycled globally
🐛

Why Waxworms?

🐛

Galleria mellonella
Greater Wax Moth

🔄 Life Cycle

🥚 → 🐛 → 🦋

Egg → Larva (feeds on beeswax) → Pupa → Adult moth
Complete cycle: ~6-15 weeks

These beehive pests naturally eat beeswax – but surprisingly, they can also break down plastic!

💡 Key Discovery (2022): Scientists found enzymes in waxworm saliva that can break down polyethylene plastic in just hours at room temperature!

Phenol oxidase enzyme

What Did We Study?

We analyzed the waxworm genome to look for genes that may help break down plastic.

🧠
Think of the genome like an instruction manual

It contains all the genetic instructions that make an organism work. By reading this manual, we can find the specific genes responsible for plastic breakdown.

🔬

What is Genome Annotation?

🗺️

It's like labeling parts of a map

Genome annotation means finding genes in DNA and understanding what those genes do.

Finding genes in DNA
Understanding gene functions
Labeling each part
Creating a reference guide
⚙️

Our Process

1

🧪Analyzed genome data

Examined the raw DNA sequence from waxworms

2

🧬Predicted genes (using Maker)

Used computer tools to identify where genes are located

3

📊Checked quality (BUSCO)

Verified our results using standard quality tests

4

🔍Viewed genes in genome browser

Visualized the annotated genome to confirm findings

📊

What We Found

~3,682 Genes identified
High Gene completeness
Quality Annotation validated
